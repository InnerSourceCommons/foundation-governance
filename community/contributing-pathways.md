# Contributing Pathways
 
This guide is for anyone who wants to contribute to InnerSource Commons, irrespective of whether you have been a practitioner for years, have just discovered InnerSource, work in research, or are simply curious about the community. All contributions are welcome!
 
> **A note before you begin**
> 
> Contributing to InnerSource Commons does not mean writing code. ISC is a community of practice. Its value comes from shared knowledge, lived experience, thoughtful collaboration, and the people who show up, in whatever capacity they can.
 
## Who Contributes?
 
InnerSource Commons brings together people with different relationships to InnerSource. Contributors include practitioners applying open collaboration principles inside their organizations, ISPO professionals responsible for enabling and scaling InnerSource across their teams, and academics and researchers studying the ecosystem and the organizations within it.
 
Each of these groups arrives with different questions and different things to offer. What they share is an interest in advancing how InnerSource is understood and practiced and a willingness to contribute that understanding back to the community.
 
There is no single profile of a contributor here. If you are working with InnerSource in any capacity and have something to offer, this is the right place.
 
## Ways to Contribute
 
The table below outlines the main contribution areas at InnerSource Commons. Use it as a starting point, not a constraint. Many of the most impactful contributions happen at the intersection of these areas.
 
| Contribution Area | What this looks like |
| --- | --- |
| **Community Participation** | Attending calls, asking questions, sharing your perspective, speaking at the Summit or community calls, and contributing content to the monthly newsletter. |
| **Knowledge & Practice** | Writing or co-authoring InnerSource patterns, contributing case studies, translating materials, or creating original content in your language. |
| **Review & Quality** | Reviewing patterns or resources for clarity, accuracy, and completeness. Asking the questions that surface gaps. Improving what already exists. |
| **Research** | Contributing academic or applied research findings, connecting scholarly work to InnerSource patterns, or co-authoring research with community members. |
| **Communications & Advocacy** | Writing blog posts, sharing community work on social media or at conferences, or acting as an InnerSource Ambassador in your organization or region. |
| **Events & Gatherings** | Helping organize local Gatherings or the InnerSource Summit, speaking at events, supporting logistics, or sharing outcomes back with the community. |
| **Design & Accessibility** | Creating visuals or presentation materials, improving resource usability, and reviewing community outputs for accessibility and inclusion. |
 
## A Closer Look at each Area
 
### Community Participation
 
Participation is a contribution. InnerSource Commons runs on the knowledge and experience of people who engage actively: asking questions, sharing what they know, and adding their perspective to the conversations that shape what the community produces.
 
You can contribute by:
 
- Attending community calls, webinars, or virtual coffee sessions
- Sharing your perspective or asking a question during a discussion
- Speaking at the InnerSource Summit or a community call
- Contributing content to the monthly community newsletter
- Participating in #virtual-coffee-buddies to connect with other community members one-on-one
### Knowledge and Practice
 
InnerSource Commons exists to capture and share real-world experience with InnerSource. Contributions here ensure that what the community produces stays practical, relevant, and grounded in how InnerSource actually works across different organizational contexts.
 
You can contribute by:
 
- Writing or co-authoring InnerSource patterns
- Sharing a case study from your organisation's InnerSource journey
- Translating existing community materials into your language
- Creating original InnerSource resources in a language other than English
- Helping convert practitioner experience into reusable guidance
Contributions in any language are equally valued. If you are more comfortable working in Japanese, French, Brazilian Portuguese, Chinese, or any other language, you do not need to translate your work into English first. The community welcomes original contributions as they are.
 
### Review and Quality
 
Not all contributions involve creating something new. Reviewing and strengthening existing work is just as important as starting anew.
 
You can contribute by:
 
- Reviewing patterns or resources for accuracy, clarity, and completeness
- Flagging outdated content or broken links
- Providing structured feedback on drafts before publication
- Asking the questions that surface unstated assumptions or gaps
- Helping ensure consistency in tone, terminology, and style across resources
Rigorous, constructive review directly improves the quality and credibility of everything the community publishes.
 
### Research
 
InnerSource Commons serves researchers as well as practitioners. Academic and applied research findings help ground the community's outputs in evidence, identify patterns across organizations, and connect InnerSource to adjacent fields.
 
You can contribute by:
 
- Sharing research findings relevant to InnerSource in community discussions or publications
- Co-authoring research with community members or working groups
- Connecting academic literature to existing InnerSource patterns
- Contributing to the [ISC Research section](https://innersourcecommons.org/learn/research/) with studies, data, or analysis
- Helping practitioner-generated knowledge meet scholarly standards
If you are a researcher, your work belongs here. You do not need to be a full-time practitioner to contribute meaningfully.
 
### Communications and Advocacy
 
InnerSource Commons has reached over 3,000 people across 800+ organizations in part because community members tell the story. Outreach, writing, and advocacy help the community grow and ensure InnerSource knowledge reaches people who need it.
 
You can contribute by:
 
- Writing or editing blog posts for the ISC website
- Speaking about InnerSource at external conferences or meetups
- Sharing community publications and resources on social media
- Acting as an InnerSource Ambassador in your organisation, region, or industry
- Submitting articles to the community newsletter via this [GitHub issue](https://github.com/InnerSourceCommons/InnerSourceMarketing/issues/new/choose)
### Events and Gatherings
 
Community events are how InnerSource Commons brings people together across geographies, time zones, and organizations. Event contributions extend the community's reach and create the conditions for collaboration that happens nowhere else.
 
You can contribute by:
 
- Helping organise a local or regional InnerSource Gathering in your city or country
- Supporting the InnerSource Summit as a speaker, co-organiser, or volunteer
- Assisting with event logistics, facilitation, or follow-up documentation
- Applying to speak at a Gathering or community call
- Sharing what you learned after attending a session with the community

### Design and Accessibility
 
Clear, accessible communication is essential for a global community that spans many languages, cultures, ability levels, and connectivity contexts. Contributions in this area help ensure everyone can access and use what the community produces.
 
You can contribute by:
 
- Designing visuals, diagrams, or presentation materials for community events or publications
- Reviewing community outputs for readability and plain-language clarity
- Improving accessibility for people with visual, auditory, or cognitive disabilities
- Ensuring resources work in low-bandwidth or low-resource environments
- Flagging content that assumes cultural familiarity that not all readers share
Accessibility is not a final check. It is a design principle. If you notice a barrier that others may not have seen, naming it is itself a contribution.
