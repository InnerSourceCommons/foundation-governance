# Contributor Ladder in InnerSource Commons
 
InnerSource Commons follows a trust- and responsibility-based contributor model, consistent with long-standing open source communities like the Apache Software Foundation (ASF). Roles are earned through sustained participation and demonstrated commitment, not assigned by title or organizational affiliation.
 
The ladder describes four *roles*, from those who engage with and benefit from community resources, through to those who take on formal stewardship responsibilities within the Foundation. Advancing along the ladder is always optional. Each role has its own value, and no one is expected to progress beyond where their time, interest, and circumstances allow.
 
We reckon that not everyone who engages with InnerSource Commons is on a path toward contribution or governance. Vendors and organizational leaders, for example, often engage with community resources to inform their work or evaluate InnerSource for their context. This is a legitimate and valued part of the ecosystem, and the ladder accommodates that from the outset.
 
InnerSource Commons is governed by a Board of Directors that provides strategic oversight and legal accountability for the Foundation. Board membership sits outside this contributor ladder. [Learn more about the Board of Directors](https://innersourcecommons.org/about/board/)
 
It's worth noting that anyone who joins the InnerSource Commons Slack is a **community member**. This includes Foundation Members, Contributors, Board Members, and anyone joining the community for the first time. Community membership is the foundation for everything else.
 
## Observer
 
**Who they are**
 
Observers are community members who have joined the InnerSource Commons Slack and are in a quiet, absorbing mode. They are present but not yet actively participating in discussions, calls, or contributions, and we recognize and value this stage in the community journey.
 
**What they do**
 
- Read and apply InnerSource patterns, case studies, and learning resources
- Watch community call recordings and follow discussions without engaging
- Join working group meetings or channels occasionally
- Engage with community outputs to inform their own work or thinking
- Follow InnerSource Commons on social media and stay across announcements

**How they add value**
 
Observers grow the reach of InnerSource Commons by quietly absorbing and applying community knowledge in their own contexts. Their presence reflects the relevance of the community's work, and many of the community's most active contributors began as observers.
 
## Participant or Users
 
**Who they are**
 
Participants or users are individuals and companies who engage with InnerSource Commons to learn, apply, research, or evaluate InnerSource practices. This includes practitioners, researchers, students, managers, open source professionals, organizational leaders assessing InnerSource for their context, and vendors engaging with the ecosystem to better serve their customers.
 
They show up in discussions, attend events, and interact with others in the community. Participation does not require making a formal contribution; it is about being present and showing up in your own way.
 
**What they do**
 
- Attend community calls, InnerSource Gatherings, or the Summit
- Share resources informally within their organisations or networks
- Engage with community outputs to inform internal decision-making or product development
- Review or comment on documents, proposals, or discussions
- Provide feedback or share experiences without long-term commitment

**Expectations**
 
There are no formal responsibilities at this tier. No time commitment is required and there is no obligation to contribute. Users are welcome to engage as much or as little as their needs allow.
 
**How they add value**
 
Participants bring energy and new perspectives to the community. Their questions surface gaps in existing knowledge, their discussions generate ideas, and their presence makes the community feel alive. Participation is often what draws people toward deeper contribution.

## Contributor
 
**Who they are**
 
Contributors are individuals who actively help move the community forward by participating in the creation, improvement, or dissemination of work produced by InnerSource Commons. Contributing is open to anyone, and no formal approval is required to begin.
 
**What they do**
 
- Contribute to InnerSource patterns or case studies
- Review and improve existing content for accuracy, clarity, or completeness
- Help with documentation, research summaries, or learning materials
- Support working group activities
- Assist with events, communications, or community initiatives
- Speak at community calls or InnerSource Gatherings
- Contribute content to the monthly community newsletter
**Expectations**
 
- Follow the community [Code of Conduct](https://innersourcecommons.org/about/codeofconduct/)
- Engage respectfully and constructively on our Slack and GitHub issues/discussions
- Participate in discussions on groups or activities they're involved with

**How they add value**
 
Contributors turn ideas and experience into tangible outputs. They are the primary engine of the community's knowledge production and the foundation upon which everything else is built.
 
**How to progress**
 
Contributing is self-directed. Anyone can begin contributing at any time by engaging with a working group, submitting a pattern, or participating in community initiatives.
 
There is no application process to become a Contributor. Sustained, high-quality contribution over time is what opens the path toward Foundation Membership.

## Foundation Member
 
**Who they are**
 
Foundation Members are contributors who have demonstrated sustained, high-quality engagement with the community and have been recognized through a formal nomination and election process. Membership is purely merit-based, free of any cost, and restricted to individuals. It is not tied to the organization a person works for or their professional title.
 
Foundation Membership also opens the path to becoming a Trusted Committer. **Trusted Committer** is a stewardship role for Foundation Members who take on formal leadership of a working group or initiative. It is documented separately in the InnerSource Commons [Bylaws](https://innersourcecommons.org/about/board/).
 
**What they do**
 
- Uphold the Foundation's governance and mission
- Participate in formal decision-making processes
- Vote in Board elections and in the election of new Foundation Members
- Participate in the members' mailing list
- Support the long-term sustainability of the community

**Expectations**
 
- Ongoing commitment to the Foundation's values and Code of Conduct
- Willingness to engage in governance processes
- Accountability to the broader community
**How they add value**
 
Foundation Members bridge the community and governance layers of InnerSource Commons. Their participation ensures that institutional decisions remain grounded in community reality and that the Foundation remains accountable to the people doing the work.
 
**How to become a Foundation Member**
 
- Build a track record of sustained, meaningful contributions to the community/ecosystem. Contributions can include, but not be limited to:
  - Speaking about InnerSource at an ecosystem event
  - Contributing to a working group
  - Presenting at an ISC community call
  - Supporting activities within the commons
- Be nominated by a current Foundation Member who can speak to your contributions.
- The nomination is put to a vote by the existing Foundation Membership.
- Upon confirmation, you will be welcomed as a Foundation Member and added to the members list, and legally become a part owner of the Foundation.
- Self-nominations are also welcome. If you believe your contributions reflect the level of sustained engagement the community values, you can submit a nomination directly via the Foundation Member nomination form. _(no existing form/page found on the site — needs a URL)_
