# New to the Commons? Start here
 
## STEP 1: Connect With The Community
 
InnerSource Commons uses Slack for real-time conversations. All discussions are governed under the [Chatham House Rule](https://www.chathamhouse.org/about-us/chatham-house-rule), which means you are free to use information shared in any channel, but you may not attribute it to a specific person without their permission. This creates a safe space for candid questions and honest conversation.
 
Within Slack, the #general channel is the main space for community conversations, foundation updates, and InnerSource news. Everyone is welcome to post here. You will also find working group channels, special interest groups, and regional channels as you explore.
 
For conversations the community wants to preserve long-term, we use GitHub Discussions.
 
Subscribe to the InnerSource Commons monthly newsletter for updates on community activities, working groups, and opportunities to get involved. Follow us on social media to keep up with announcements, events, and new resources as they are published.

## STEP 2: Learn What InnerSource Commons Is (and Is Not)
 
InnerSource Commons is a community of practice, not a software product or a codebase. It exists to support people and organizations applying open collaboration principles to internal software development.
 
The Foundation provides structure and sustainability for this work. If you are using InnerSource Commons resources, referencing them in your work, or sharing them with others, you are already part of the Commons.

## STEP 3: Explore InnerSource Commons Resources
 
The community produces a growing body of shared resources grounded in real-world InnerSource experience across organizations of all sizes and industries. These include:
 
- InnerSource Patterns: documented best practices in a reusable, structured format
- Case Studies: firsthand accounts of how organisations have applied InnerSource
- The Learning Path: a curated series of articles and videos covering InnerSource fundamentals
- Books: several free titles covering different aspects of InnerSource practice
- Research: academic and applied studies on InnerSource and open collaboration

## STEP 4: Attend ISC Community Calls
 
InnerSource Commons hosts regular community calls that are open to everyone and require no prior contribution to attend.
 
Community calls are presentation-led sessions with an interactive Q&A at the end. They are designed to share knowledge, experience, or case studies related to InnerSource and are a good way to understand how InnerSource is practiced across different organizations.

## STEP 5: Become A Contributor
 
When you feel comfortable, you may choose to contribute more actively. The work at InnerSource Commons is largely volunteer-led. No one, including officers and board members, is paid to contribute except for a small number of dedicated Foundation staff.
 
Contributions can take many forms and do not require formal approval to begin. They might look like:
 
- Sharing feedback on existing resources
- Amplifying community work by sharing posts or talks in the ISC Slack or on your own platforms
- Helping others discover InnerSource Commons
- Contributing to Patterns, Case Studies, or sharing your InnerSource story

 ### Conclusion
Contributing to InnerSource Commons means adding to a body of knowledge that practitioners, researchers, and organizations across the world draw on to do their work differently. If you are uncertain where your experience fits best, that is a reasonable place to be. The working groups, community calls, and Slack channels are all good ways to get a sense of where the gaps are and where your contribution would have the most impact.
 
When you are ready, introduce yourself in the [#introductions](https://innersourcecommons.slack.com/archives/C0867TAF63H) channel on Slack. If you would like a more direct conversation about where to begin, reach out to the Community Manager or an admin on Slack.
 
We also want this guide to keep improving. If something here was unclear, missing, or could be explained better, we would love to hear it to help us improve it for the next person.
